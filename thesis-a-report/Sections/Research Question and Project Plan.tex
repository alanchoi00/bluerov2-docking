\section{Research Question and Project Plan} \label{sec:research-question}

\subsection{Primary Research Question}

\begin{quote}
    \emph{What is the feasibility and performance of a vision-based, turbidity-adaptive pose estimation system for autonomous docking of an ROV to a free-floating underwater station?}
\end{quote}

\subsection{Sub-Questions}

The primary question can be decomposed into several sub-questions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item How robust are fiducial-based (AprilTag/ArUco) and learning-based (YOLO) detection methods under varying turbidity levels, lighting conditions and relative orientations between ROV and dock?
    \item What pose estimation accuracy and update rate are required for safe docking, given the relative motion of a free-floating dock and the dynamic response of the ROV and tether?
    \item How can motion compensation and control laws be designed to account for dock motion while still precisely maneuvering to the docking pose?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Hypothesis and Expected Feasibility}

The working hypothesis for this project is:

\begin{quote}
    \emph{A turbidity-adaptive, two-level vision pipeline (light cues for long-range guidance and markers for close-range precision), combined with appropriate motion compensation and simple fallback beacons, can achieve reliable short-range docking of an ROV to a free-floating underwater station in conditions of moderate turbidity.}
\end{quote}

In particular, it is expected that a combination of image enhancement, robust fiducial detection, light-based guidance  will provide accurate pose estimates to guide the ROV through the final approach and alignment phase. However, high turbidity levels that completely blocked camera's visibility are  beyond the capability of vision alone. In such cases, hybrid sensing (e.g.\ acoustic or EM navigation system) or manual operator intervention will likely be required.

\subsection{Proposed Methodology Overview}

The proposed methodology consists of three main elements: perception, pose estimation and control.

\paragraph{Perception and Dataset Collection}

The perception pipeline will be developed from the outset using real images from the ROV camera system. Initially, a benchtop camera rig and small tank setup will be used to capture images of the docking station mock-up equipped with LEDs and ArUco/AprilTag markers under varying distances, orientations and lighting. As hardware becomes available, the BlueROV camera will be used to collect sequences in a pool, including both static and gently moving dock configurations.

An image pre-processing stage will be implemented to perform basic filtering and thresholding to enhance marker and light visibility. This module will be tuned empirically using these real datasets under different turbidity levels to balance noise suppression against improved marker and light visibility.

\paragraph{Two-Level Vision Guidance and Pose Estimation}

On top of pre-processing, a two-level detection pipeline will be implemented:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Level I (light-cue guidance):} detection of LED arrays for long-range, coarse guidance, using a lightweight CNN (YOLO) for robust light-cluster localisation.
    \item \textbf{Level II (marker guidance):} detection of passive AprilTag/ArUco markers mounted on the dock for close-range, high-precision guidance, using established python libraries and a CNN-based ROI detector as backup under degraded conditions.
\end{itemize}

Pose estimation will be performed via Perspective-\emph{n}-Point (PnP) solvers with RANSAC outlier rejection, using known 3D geometry of the light cluster and marker and their 2D image measurements. Separate light-based and marker-based pose estimates will be fused in a temporal filtering framework (e.g.\ a Kalman or complementary filter) to provide a smooth, confidence-weighted relative pose. All accuracy and robustness metrics (e.g.\ position/orientation error, detection range, latency) will be quantified using real imagery with ground-truth or pseudo ground-truth measurements (e.g.\ calibrated test rigs and known distances).

\paragraph{Control and Motion Compensation (Simulation-Focused).}

Control and motion-compensation strategies will be developed primarily in Gazebo/ROS~2, using an abstract “pose sensor” that provides relative dock pose to the simulated ROV. The noise characteristics, latency and dropout behaviour of this sensor will be configured based on statistics measured from the real-image perception pipeline.

In sim, the free-floating dock will be modelled with simple sway or heave motion to emulate cable-suspended behaviour. Using the abstract pose feed, a closed-loop controller will be designed to manage:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \emph{Coarse approach} along the dock axis using Level~I pose primarily;
    \item \emph{Fine alignment} and position hold using Level~II pose primarily;
    \item \emph{Final engagement} into the docking structure within allowable tolerances;
    \item \emph{Abort and fallback behaviours} when pose confidence or update rate drops below thresholds.
\end{itemize}

Controller gains, mode-switching logic and safety conditions will be tuned entirely in Gazebo until repeatable simulated docking is achieved over a range of initial conditions and dock motions. Because the perception stack is not simulated, this stage isolates the control problem from rendering artifacts and keeps simulation focused on dynamics.

\paragraph{Integration with the Physical BlueROV}

Once both components are mature, the perception and pose-estimation stack will be deployed on the BlueROV platform and connected to the control loop. Initial tests will be conducted with conservative behaviours (slow approach, generous abort margins) in a pool with a static dock, before progressing to experiments with a gently moving, suspended dock. Experimental results will then be used to refine both the pose error model (fed back into simulation) and the controller tuning.

\subsection{Timeline}

The core technical development of this project will be undertaken during Thesis~B and Thesis~C. Thesis~A is limited to preliminary work such as basic ArUco-based pose estimation on real images, basic CAD modelling of the docking station and detailed planning.

\subsubsection*{Thesis B}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Weeks 1--2: Perception pipeline consolidation.}
          Finalise the basic image acquisition and pre-processing pipeline using the ROV camera and benchtop/tank setups. Implement and test the two-level detection scheme (Level~I light cues, Level~II markers) on real underwater images, and verify that PnP-based pose estimation runs reliably offline.

    \item \textbf{Weeks 3--4: Quantitative evaluation on real imagery.}
          Collect structured datasets in a pool or tank with a static dock under varying distances, orientations, turbidity and lighting. Quantify detection range, pose accuracy (position/orientation error) and latency for both light-based and marker-based guidance. Derive empirical statistics for pose noise, dropout and update rates.

    \item \textbf{Weeks 4--6: Control development in simulation (Gazebo/ROS2).}
          Configure a simplified Gazebo scenario with a BlueROV-style model and the designed free-floating dock. Design and tune the docking controller (coarse approach, fine alignment, abort/fallback logic).

    \item \textbf{Weeks 6--8: Initial integration and short pool trials.}
          Deploy the perception and pose-estimation stack on the ROV compute platform and connect its output to a conservative version of the controller. Perform short pool trials with a static dock to verify that real-time pose feeds satisfy the assumptions used in simulation (e.g.\ update rate and noise bounds) and that basic approach/alignment behaviours are stable.

    \item \textbf{Week 9: Reporting and planning for Thesis C.}
          Finalise the Thesis~B report, summarising: (i) perception performance on real underwater imagery; (ii) controller performance in Gazebo under realistic pose-feed emulation; and (iii) identified gaps and risks for full integrated docking in Thesis~C.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Thesis C}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Weeks 1--3: Full-system integration and static-dock docking.}
          Refine the integration between perception, state estimation and control on the physical ROV. Conduct systematic pool trials with a static dock to demonstrate repeatable short-range docking, measure success rate and time-to-dock, and adjust safety margins and abort thresholds.

    \item \textbf{Weeks 3--6: Free-floating dock experiments.}
          Introduce a suspended or otherwise movable dock to emulate free-floating behaviour (e.g.\ cable-suspended frame with induced sway). Evaluate how the system handles relative motion and tether effects, and refine motion-compensation strategies if required. Update the pose-error and delay model and, if necessary, retune the controller in Gazebo.

    \item \textbf{Weeks 6--8: Robustness and envelope characterisation.}
          Perform extended test campaigns with multiple initial conditions, repeated runs, and varied turbidity/lighting levels. Quantify docking success rate, time-to-dock, and any failure modes. Identify the operational envelope (e.g.\ maximum turbidity, allowable dock motion) within which vision-based docking remains reliable.

    \item \textbf{Weeks 8--10: Final analysis, recommendations and reporting.}
          Consolidate all experimental results, compare simulated and real-world behaviour, and formulate design recommendations for vision-based ROV docking to free-floating stations. Complete the Thesis~C report and supporting artefacts (figures, videos, code documentation) for submission and potential future extension.
\end{itemize}

The exact week boundaries may be adjusted according to equipment availability and academic calendar constraints.
